The Paradox of Popular Participation
If not for the devastating human consequences that always ensue, the early consolidation of power by Putin shows a meticulous dismantling of democratic institutions, executed with such calculated precision that one might almost admire its technical skill. In 2001, Epstein, Knight, and Shvetsova (2001) reported that judicial independence was being undermined by a practice called "practiced calibration." This meant that constitutional protections were being transformed into procedural tasks instead of actual checks on power. From judiciary to judicial theater, this transformation illustrates how institutional erosion often begins with subtle hollowing, like termites methodically consuming a structure while leaving its façade intact.

This pattern, established in Russia with almost textbook precision, has manifested across multiple contexts with the reliability of being joined by academic funding cuts and administrative promises of support. In the forensic examination of the 2018 elections, Bangladesh is a particularly interesting case study. 
The ruling Bangladesh Awami League orchestrated what Riaz and Parvez (2021) documented as a comprehensive strategy of electoral control, involving civilian administration capture, law enforcement deployment, and electoral commission co-option. This triumvirate of institutional manipulation resulted in the mathematically improbable outcome of 96% of votes for the ruling party.

The transformation of modernization tools into control instruments follows a predictable pattern, rather like watching promising  students abandon their intellectual curiosity for administrative ambitions. The Digital Security Act, as analyzed by Miaji and Islam (2023, p. 367), demonstrates the transformation of security legislation into mechanisms of systemic oppression through meticulous rhetorical framing. The provisions regarding "anti-state activities" and "deterioration of law and order" create expansive linguistic vessels that can be stuffed with whatever content those in power find inconvenient, a legislative Rorschach test where authorities consistently identify precisely the threats they wish to eradicate. 

Rahman and Rashid (2020) show that these vague rules have been used against journalists with the same precision as hiring committees using "excellence" criteria to choose the best candidates.

Bangladesh's case merits scholarly attention because it represents what Eisenstadt, Levan, and Maboudi (2017) might characterize as an experimental space for authoritarian innovation—a laboratory where democratic forms are maintained while democratic substance is methodically extracted, rather than akin to meetings to justify more meetings. JusticeCraft as a Framework for Democratic Renewal
The legal barriers to democracy in Bangladesh require innovative approaches that recognize both the sophisticated nature of modern authoritarian legal frameworks and the potential for transformative action. Balasco et al. (2024) have developed the JusticeCraft framework, which offers a promising conceptual and practical approach for addressing these challenges. Unlike traditional transitional justice approaches that often prescribe linear, institutionally focused interventions, JusticeCraft represents a substantive reconceptualization of how justice operates in transitionally complex environments.
The framework developed as a response to what Balasco and colleagues termed "the inadequacy of linear, top-down approaches to transitional justice"—a diplomatic academic euphemism for the spectacular failures of conventional methods when confronted with the adaptive mechanisms of modern authoritarian systems. 
Beyond mere theoretical innovation, JusticeCraft provides analytical tools specifically calibrated for contexts where legal mechanisms themselves have become instruments of democratic erosion.
A nuanced framework for understanding how justice can be reimagined and reconstructed in contexts where formal legal systems have become barriers to democracy is provided by JusticeCraft. These three dimensions are deliberately connected instead of being separated, reflecting the messy reality of democratic struggles rather than the neat theoretical progressions that rarely survive contact with political complexity.
JusticeCraft recognizes the necessity of challenging unfair legal structures, not as acts of lawlessness but as a demonstration of commitment to broader principles of justice. In Bangladesh, this could take the form of strategic litigation against unconstitutional provisions, documentation of rights abuses under the Digital Security Act, or coordination of civil society opposition to legal overreach. This dimension acknowledges the paradoxical necessity of sometimes working against legal structures to ultimately enhance rule of law, a contradiction that would have intrigued Dostoyevsky. The Malaysian experience illustrates how persistent resistance across diverse sectors of society can ultimately overcome seemingly entrenched legal obstacles.
The reclamation aspect involves restoring democratic principles and practices that have been obscured or corrupted by authoritarian legal maneuverings. Reclamation, unlike traditional strategies that focus on restoring institutions, acknowledges that some entities may require a radical reimagining rather than merely rehabilitating. 
For Bangladesh, this entails reclaiming constitutional values that predated authoritarian amendments, emphasizing judicial independence as a fundamental principle rather than a procedural formality, and restoring notions of political accountability that have been consistently undermined. 
Particularly relevant to Bangladesh's context is the resilience dimension of JusticeCraft. Building democratic resilience requires creating institutional redundancies and social networks that can withstand legal attacks on democratic space. This lesson has been painfully learned from previous democratic transitions that collapsed when key institutions were compromised. 
This encompasses the creation of alternative media platforms that can function despite digital restrictions, the establishment of professional solidarity networks among lawyers and judges, and the establishment of cross-border connections with democratic movements confronting similar obstacles.
Importantly, JusticeCraft acknowledges that all three processes must work together and simultaneously. Resistance without reclamation runs the risk of nihilism, reclamation without resistance can become nostalgic, and neither can succeed without building up strength against inevitable failure. By incorporating these dimensions, the JusticeCraft framework provides a holistic approach to addressing Bangladesh's legal barriers to democracy.
Justice Craft is distinguished from its theoretical predecessors by its emphasis on the materiality of justice—how abstract principles become concrete practices and institutions. In Bangladesh's context, this implies transcending purely theoretical calls for democratic reform to address specific legal provisions such as Article 70 of the Constitution, which restricts parliamentary autonomy, the ambiguous provisions of the Digital Security Act, and the preventive detention laws that facilitate political repression. By concentrating on these tangible manifestations of injustice, reform efforts can precisely target leverage points within the legal framework.The importance of JusticeCraft's emphasis on knowledge production cannot be overstated.

 In Bangladesh, the manipulation of legal frameworks has been accompanied by an ideational capture—control over how justice and democracy are conceptualized and discussed. This requires the creation of alternative knowledge networks capable of articulating principled critiques of legal manipulation and developing contextually appropriate reform proposals. Universities, think tanks, and professional associations all play important roles in this process.

The student protests the quota system show both the potential and limitations of resistance without a JusticeCraft approach. The protests made people aware of unfair treatment, but lasting change requires new ways of doing things, new laws, and strong networks that can handle tough situations. These movements can be expanded from moments of protest to sustainable processes of democratic renewal with the JusticeCraft framework.

Based on comparative experiences from countries that have successfully challenged legal barriers to democracy, several specific applications of the JusticeCraft framework emerge for Bangladesh. The creation of cross-sectoral coalitions is a crucial strategy that can unite legal professionals, students, journalists, and other civil society actors around specific legal reforms. 
This strategy is based on the successful alliances that successfully challenged Malaysia's political impasse in 2018. The development of "democracy resilience hubs" is another promising way to create spaces where abuses can be documented, resistance strategies can be coordinated, and reform proposals can be made even under strict conditions.
Transnational advocacy networks are important links between Bangladesh's democratic actors and international allies, legal resources, and protection mechanisms. These networks have shown resilience in preserving democratic space when domestic options become severely constrained. Reclaiming constitutional principles through strategic litigation, public education, and professional solidarity among judges and lawyers is an essential approach, employing legal mechanisms to challenge systems that have weaponized. 
Finally, youth engagement, civic education, and mentorship programs are a long-term strategy for transmitting democratic values even in hostile legal environments.
These interlocking strategies, viewed through the JusticeCraft framework, acknowledge that addressing Bangladesh's legal barriers to democracy requires broad-based participation and institutional innovation rather than merely technical solutions. The integration of resistance, reclamation, and resilience within the framework offers Bangladesh a conceptual framework for transforming legal barriers into foundations for democratic renewal.
Breaking the cycle of pessimistic expectations is a crucial challenge, as Svolik (2013) suggests in his analysis of democratic trajectories. The JusticeCraft framework emphasizes concrete action, principled resistance, and institutional rebuilding, which is a promising approach for restoring belief in democratic possibility. The framework gives both conceptual clarity and practical guidance for the difficult but essential work of democratic reconstruction in confronting Bangladesh's sophisticated legal barriers to democracy.
